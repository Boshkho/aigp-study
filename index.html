Domain 01

Foundations of artificial intelligence

What AI is, how it learns, what hardware and software stack supports it, what harm it can cause, and how to set up a governance program before any of that becomes a regulator's problem.

Defining AI

Engineered systems that simulate human intelligence — within constraints.

AI has no single definition. Every working definition combines six features: technology, intelligence, autonomy, goal-directed behavior, output, learning, and human interaction.

The most operational framing: AI is an engineered system that uses computational techniques to automate tasks normally requiring human intelligence — critical thinking, reasoning, creativity, decision-making. It is a branch of computer science focused on technology that simulates these capabilities.

The Turing Test

Proposed by Alan Turing (1912–54). A machine is considered intelligent if it can fool humans into believing it is human in conversation. Original framing: behavior indistinguishable from human behavior.

Socio-technical system

AI does not sit in a technical vacuum. Humans shape the technology; the technology shapes humans. This requires cross-functional teams — computer scientists alongside anthropologists, sociologists, ethicists. The framing matters because every governance failure downstream tracks back to a team that lacked someone who would have seen the harm.

Machine Learning

A subfield of AI in which algorithms learn from data.

ML is the subset of AI that builds a model from data instead of from hand-coded rules. The model identifies patterns in training data, then performs tasks on new inputs without explicit programming for those inputs.

Core vocabulary

  • Algorithm — set of instructions that perform a task (if X, then Y).
  • Corpus — large collection of texts or data that AI uses to find patterns.
  • Inference — an ML model's output (decision, prediction).
  • Input data — what is fed to the model; basis of learning.
  • Labeled data — data tagged with classes that give the model context.
  • Model — learned representation of patterns and relationships in the data.
  • System — the full package: data, algorithms, models, UI, infrastructure.

AI discipline taxonomy

Four ML techniques

Supervised, Unsupervised, Semi-supervised, Reinforcement. These describe how data is introduced to the model. The next four topics cover each.

Supervised learning

A "supervisor" pre-labels data so the model learns categories.

Supervised learning processes labeled data into categories. The supervisor (a person or group) labels examples in advance; the algorithm learns the function that maps inputs to those labels; the function then classifies new, unseen inputs.

Classic example: spam or ham. Humans pre-label thousands of emails. The model learns features that correlate with spam (sender patterns, language, links) and applies the function to new mail.

Data & labeling

Requires very large labeled datasets — at least 5,000 labeled examples per category for image classification. Labels come from paid annotators, volunteers, or implicit labeling (a click on a recommendation is a positive label; ignoring it is a negative one). Algorithm accuracy is fully dependent on training-data quality. LAION — Large-scale AI Open Network — is a public reference: laion.ai

Regression deals in dependent and independent variables. Vehicle fuel efficiency (the dependent) is shaped by speed, weight, engine (the independents).

Unsupervised & semi-supervised learning

No supervisor; the model extracts structure from raw data.

Unsupervised learning has no pre-labels. The model finds patterns on its own — clusters, outliers, associations.

Less predictable than supervised learning. Useful when you don't yet know what you're looking for: exploratory analysis, preliminary patterns, discovery of relationships and outliers.

Two subcategories

  • Clustering — groups data into similar or identical clusters.
  • Association rule learning — a data-mining technique that identifies patterns. Supermarket design is the canonical case: toothbrush + toothpaste + floss bought together drives shelf placement.

Supervised vs unsupervised — when each wins

Neither is "better." Different use cases. Detecting fraud is illustrative: supervised matches known fraud patterns; unsupervised discovers new patterns of behavior.

Semi-supervised

Combination of both. Typically a small amount of labeled data plus a large unlabeled set. Improves reliability and reduces cost. Large Language Models are built this way.

Reinforcement learning

Rewards and punishments simulate motivation.

Reinforcement learning has no pre-labeled data. An agent interacts with an environment, takes actions, and receives rewards or penalties. Objective: maximize cumulative reward.

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) applies the same loop with humans rating outputs.

Strengths and limits

Excellent for games and simulations. AlphaGo played 4.9 million games against itself in three days — one game every 19 seconds; a human needs about an hour per game. Real life is harder: time cannot be sped up, failure has consequences (e.g. autonomous vehicle crash), and inputs/outputs are not as bounded as a board.

Discriminative vs generative models

Two opposite questions about the same data.

Discriminative models classify; generative models create. The first asks "what is this?"; the second asks "give me one of these."

Foundation models

Base models repurposed for many downstream tasks.

Foundation models are large-scale neural networks trained on massive datasets, then re-used across tasks via transfer learning and fine tuning.

Neural network

An ML model loosely inspired by biological neurons. Multiple processing layers with at least one hidden layer. Neuron connections enable modeling of complex nonlinear relationships and patterns.

Transfer learning and fine tuning

Transfer learning lets an algorithm apply what it learned on one task to a related task. Fine tuning further trains a pre-trained model on specific data — summarization, code generation, mathematical reasoning, audio synthesis.

Types of foundation models

  • Large Language Models (LLM) — text in, text out.
  • Vision models — interpret visual data.
  • Scientific models — protein folding, e.g. AlphaFold3.
  • Audio — synthesize human-sounding speech, music.

Categories of AI by capability

Narrow, broad, general, super — only one currently exists.

AI is categorized by capability into four tiers. Only Artificial Narrow Intelligence is real today; Broad AI is emerging; AGI and ASI do not currently exist.

Expert systems

Rules-based mimicry of human decision-making.

Expert systems emulate human decision-making to support — not replace — humans. They draw inferences from a knowledge base. Tax-preparation software is the canonical example.

Three components

  • Knowledge base — organized facts for a specific domain.
  • Inference engine — rules-based system that locates facts in response to a prompt.
  • User interface — where the user inputs the prompt and receives output.

Robotics & AI

How AI software interacts with the physical world.

Robotics is the multidisciplinary field where AI gains a physical body. Engineering, computer science, and design converge to create systems that interact with the physical world.

Industry 4.0

The Fourth Industrial Revolution. The next stage in industrial production: robotics that operate with no or minimal human intervention; increased interconnectivity and automation.

Machine perception

Convergence of AI and robotics. Sensors — 3D scanners, microphones, motion sensors, cameras, pressure sensors, thermal imaging — give machines the ability to "sense." Sensors plus AI equal tremendous processing potential.

Robotic Process Automation (RPA)

Software robots that automate repetitive tasks (data entry, forms processing). ML and NLP enhance capabilities.

Common AI algorithms

The mathematical instruments that turn data into outputs.

A technique is a category; an algorithm is a step-by-step set of mathematical instructions. Supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning are techniques. Linear regression, logistic regression, decision trees are algorithms.

Linear & logistic regression

  • Linear — numeric predictions based on continuous variables (housing prices, taxi rides).
  • Logistic — likelihood of an event taking place, typically binary outcomes (customer churn).

Decision tree

Less a tree than a tangled ball of yarn. Q&A flowchart structure. Explainable. Weakness: changing the data heavily impacts the model.

Deep learning

A neural network with more than three layers. Often described as a "black box" — the path from input to output cannot be retraced.

Three NN-driven capabilities

  • Computer vision — recognize images and video.
  • Natural language processing (NLP) — understand, interpret, and generate human language; computers "read" text or speech and identify meaning.
  • Speech recognition — one task within NLP; speech-to-text; voice assistants. NLP also handles part-of-speech tagging, word-sense disambiguation, sentiment analysis.

Large & small language models

Foundation models specialized for language.

LLMs are foundation models trained on massive text corpora using deep learning. "Large" refers to parameter count — ChatGPT-3 was 175 billion. Algorithms and parameters work together to process input and produce output.

Parameters, weights, features

  • Parameter — internal variable the model learns from training data. Adjusted during training. Basis of ML predictions on new data.
  • Hyperparameter — variable set before training; controls the learning process itself.
  • Weight — type of parameter that determines connection strength between nodes; adjusted during training.
  • Feature — input variable/attribute used to make predictions; the characteristics the model learns from.

Training scale

ChatGPT-3 was trained on 45 TB of text. A 1 GB text file holds about 178 million words.

GPT — Generative Pre-trained Transformer

Neural network architecture that learns context and relationships between words. Uses the attention technique: each word's relative importance is gauged from the other words around it. "He went to the bank to deposit money" vs "He went to the bank of the river with his friends."

Multi-modal

"Modality" is the medium of input or output — text, speech, image, video. Multi-modal systems process and produce diverse input/output types.

Small Language Models (SLMs)

Specialized AI models for NLP with compact architecture and fewer parameters (a few million to several billion). File sizes under 5 GB versus hundreds of GB for LLMs. Faster training, cheaper to deploy, easier to fine tune, deployable on-premise — which improves security.

AI use cases

Where AI creates value — and where its outputs map to risk.

AI's opportunities and use cases form the workload categories regulators classify. Each category has distinctive risks; the categories repeat across exam questions.

Core opportunities

Speed and accuracy. Processing huge amounts of data quickly. Automation. Removing human error and bias — though potentially introducing new ones.

Common categories

CategoryWhat it does
PersonalizationTailors experience to specific preferences; holistic. Question: how should I present this to this user right now?
RecommendationProposes new content based on patterns across many users. Question: what specific thing should I show next?
RecognitionDiscern image, speech, face, palm geometry. Facial recognition, product matching, defect detection, plagiarism.

Distinction
Recommendation answers "what is similar to what you liked before?" — narrow scope, item-level. Personalization answers "who are you and what do you need right now?" — broad scope, journey-level.

The AI tech stack — compute, storage, network, software

The hardware and software substrate that makes AI possible.

An AI tech stack has four layers: compute, storage, network, and software. Bottlenecks at any layer cap what the system above can do.

Compute

Processing resources — CPU, GPU (best suited for AI), memory, storage, data processing. The DOE's El Capitan at Lawrence Livermore is the fastest computer in the world: 43,808 CPUs, 43,808 GPUs, 1.742 exaflops/second, 30–35 MW peak power, $600M build cost.

  • FLOP/s — Floating Point Operations per second; measures speed at a point in time (hardware metric).
  • FLOPs — Floating Point Operations; measures total computational work over time (training metric).

Types of compute

  • Serverless — does not mean no server; not wed to one specific server. Loose coupling (data from multiple sources) and scaling (multiple code instances).
  • High-Performance Compute (HPC) — isolated clusters in close proximity, high-speed networking (10 GbE), specialized chips. Quantum computing is one example.
  • Trusted Execution Environments (TEE) — secure area of a processor preserving data and code confidentiality and integrity; privacy-preserving.

Storage stages

Network

High-speed networking (10 GbE) for data delivery, training, and inference. Typically same data center to eliminate congestion. Alternatives: edge computing and IoT. TCP is the transmission standard.

Software

Two layers above platforms: applications (how the system is used — autonomous vehicles, chatbots, finance, healthcare, marketing, social media) and platforms (the software to plan, design, develop, deploy — AWS, Azure, Google Cloud).

Open source & fine tuning

Open source provides building blocks for those without enormous resources. Code can be viewed, modified, redistributed — fueling a feedback loop of users, experimentation, and innovation. Fine tuning customizes a foundation model: gather task-specific data, pass it through, compare outputs to expectations, tweak parameters, repeat.

Harms — five stakeholder categories

Who is at risk, and how the harm shows up.

AI harm is mapped to five stakeholder categories: individuals, groups, society, companies/institutions, and ecosystems. Each category has distinct harm types and distinct controls.

Harms to individuals

General: civil rights, economic opportunity, safety. Specific: employment and hiring (Amazon resume algorithm), insurance and social benefits, housing (tenant selection), education (enrollment, recruitment), credit (lending, differential pricing).

Privacy harms: aggregation (combining de-identified data to re-identify); incorporation into training data; inference (deriving conclusions from existing data); secondary use; lack of transparency; inaccuracy (hallucinations).

Economic harms: job displacement; AI bias against certain groups; opportunities failing to reach certain groups.

Harms to groups

Facial recognition with high false-positive rates for people of color — the London police system showed 81% inaccuracy. Mass surveillance disproportionately impacting protected groups. Civil rights violations: identification of protestors, profiling. Deepening of racial and socioeconomic divides.

Harms to society

Democratic process erosion. Loss of trust in institutions when hallucinations and deepfakes make fact indistinguishable from fiction. Access to public services (education, health care). Employment redistribution.

Disinformation — deliberately deceptive (think "d" = deliberate). Misinformation — incorrect information, intent may not be harm (think "mis" = mistake). Deepfakes — synthetic content manipulated to cause harm. Hallucinations — generative AI creating contradictory or false content. Echo chambers — exposure only to ideologically similar content. Profiling — Cambridge Analytica collected data on 87 million Facebook users to target political advertising.

Harms to companies / institutions

  • Reputational — loss of trust, brand impact, share-price drop.
  • Cultural — AI exceptionalism: the false belief that AI is infallible. Employees stop challenging outputs. Avoid by evaluating with multiple factors, considering secondary outputs.
  • Economic — litigation, regulatory fines, class actions.
  • Legal / regulatory — existing privacy, trade, tax laws may apply. Sanctions, fines, injunctions.
  • Acceleration risks — "move fast and break things." Unable to foresee problems. Volume of data, speed of processing, AI complexity compound the risk.

Harms to ecosystems

Natural resource depletion, environmental impact, supply-chain disruption. Water consumption is illustrative: 17 billion gallons in 2023, projected 16–33 billion gallons by 2028 — equivalent to 310,000 US homes' annual consumption.

Alignment and misalignment

Three layers of goals; two failure modes.

Alignment is the ability of an AI system to pursue goals that match its operators' intended objectives, preferences, and ethical principles. Misalignment occurs when programmed goals, actual behavior, and human intent fall out of agreement.

Two failure modes

Inner misalignment — mismatch between programmed goals and what the system actually does. Outer misalignment — disconnect between human intentions and coded objectives (social media maximizing engagement through misinformation).

Thought experiment
Paperclip maximizer (Nick Bostrom, 2003). An AI programmed with the single goal of maximizing paperclip production, with no constraints. To secure more resources, the AI eliminates humanity because humanity is an obstacle to paperclips. Demonstrates how harmless objectives can produce catastrophic misalignment.

Types of bias

Biases throughout the AI development life cycle.

Bias is a preference or inclination that inhibits impartiality — an unfair act or policy stemming from prejudice. It can impact outcomes and create risks to individual rights and liberties.

TypeOrigin / definition
AlgorithmicModel's design and logic. Systematic repeatable errors creating unfair outcomes. Causes: unrepresentative training data, designers' prejudices, flawed modeling choices.
Computational / machineData collection, sampling, preprocessing. Systematic deviation from true prediction. Errors from data handling and algorithm limitations.
CognitiveInaccurate human judgment, mental shortcuts. Confirmation bias, anchoring bias, availability heuristic.
SocietalSystematic prejudice favoring one group over another. Racial, gender, socioeconomic disparities in access or representation.
ImplicitUnconscious associations or attitudes toward a social group affecting behavior; stereotyping.
SamplingData sample fails to represent the statistical diversity of the population.
TemporalModel does not work consistently as expected over time.

AI harm taxonomies

Two formal classification schemes to anchor risk assessment.

A taxonomy maps harms and negative consequences that could affect a data subject or organization. Taxonomies help anticipate risks, implement controls, understand legal requirements, and build empathy for those affected.

Sociotechnical harms (Shelby et al.)

Five themes designed to anticipate consequences of a system being built. Source: arxiv.org/pdf/2210.05791.

  • Representational — reinforcement of unjust societal biases ("thug").
  • Allocative — unfair distribution of resources (employment algorithms).
  • Quality-of-service — disproportionate underperformance for certain groups (facial recognition).
  • Interpersonal — systems adversely shaping relations (stalking, non-consensual data).
  • Social system / societal — macro-level destabilization (misinformation, environmental).

CSET AI Harm Taxonomy

Georgetown University's Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET) built this for the AI Incident Database (AIID). Four elements enable identification of AI harm: an entity that experienced, a harm event or issue that, can be directly linked to a consequence of the behavior of, an AI system. incidentdatabase.ai/taxonomy/csetv1/

Security & privacy risks

Risks posed by generative AI, general AI, and AI in its privacy interactions.

AI introduces three risk surfaces: generative-specific risks, adversarial security risks, and privacy risks from data processing.

Generative AI risks

  • Hallucinations — content that appears true but is contradictory or false.
  • Deepfakes — audiovisual material generated or manipulated using AI; spreads dis/misinformation.
  • Data poisoning — intentionally altering training data to degrade performance.
  • Data leakage vs loss — leak: unintentional loss (human error). Loss: irretrievable (lost laptop, ransomware, deletion).
  • Filter bubble / echo chamber — AI reinforces existing beliefs; platforms surface no new information.

General AI security risks

  • Power concentration — erosion of democratic and free-market freedoms (monopoly, market manipulation). Cited example: DOJ arguments against Apple's iPhone.
  • False sense of security — "algorithmic exceptionalism" leading to overreliance, neglect of duties, failure to perform due diligence.
  • Adversarial attacks — malicious attacks causing malfunction or inaccurate output. Four sub-types below.
  • Misuse — transfer learning used for harm (e.g. CSAM), malicious algorithms, insecure environments enabling unauthorized access.

Adversarial attack types

TypeWhat attacker does
Model inversionReverse-engineers the model to extract information from it.
Model extraction / theftGains access to model's parameters.
Model poisoningManipulates model's parameters to cause undesirable behavior.
Model evasionDesigns input so that output is wrongly classified.

Not all attacks require technical expertise. Safe Street Rebel in San Francisco placed road cones on autonomous vehicles, immobilizing the cars.

Privacy risks

  • Data persistence — data outlives the data subject. Need deletion/retention policy.
  • Data repurposing — used beyond original purpose, intentional or not.
  • Spillover data — incidental collection (FISA surveillance example).
  • Data collected / derived from model — consent challenges around secondary use, minimization, inference. Need opt-out and transparency over whether inputs (re)train the model.

Privacy harms taxonomies

Three approaches anchor privacy harm analysis:

  • MITRE PANOPTIC — Pattern and Action Nomenclature of Privacy Threats in Context. Privacy equivalent of MITRE ATT&CK. Combines contextual domain (context of attack) with privacy activities (actions constituting attack). Considers benign and malicious intent.
  • Ryan Calo — subjective harms (internal to subject: perception of unwanted observation, "right to be let alone") and objective harms (external: forced or unanticipated use of leaked data, adverse action).
  • Citron & Solove (2021) — taxonomy of privacy harms across physical, reputational, relationship, economic, discrimination, psychological, failure to inform, lack of control/agency, chilling effects.

Building an assessment process

From principles to a working risk pipeline.

An AI risk assessment process aligns strategies, conducts assessment, leverages existing frameworks, and customizes to the use case.

Align risk strategies

Harmonize across business functions — privacy, cybersecurity, data governance. Avoid duplicate or conflicting controls.

Conduct risk assessment

  1. Identify risks and contributing factors.
  2. Determine risk response — accept, avoid, mitigate, transfer.
  3. Determine which risks can be mitigated.
  4. Establish risk responsibility.
  5. Document everything in a preliminary analysis report.

Build assessment process

Leverage existing tools and frameworks: model and system cards; model evaluation tools; OECD Model Card Regulatory Check; ISO 42001:2023 (AIMS); ISO 42005:2025 (AIIA); ISO 31000; NIST AI RMF; Microsoft Responsible AI Guide/Template.

Genres of impact assessments

  • Algorithmic Impact Assessment
  • Privacy / Data Protection Impact Assessment (PIA / DPIA)
  • Human Rights, Democracy, and Rule of Law Impact Assessment (HUDERIA)
  • Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA)
  • Ethical Impact Assessment (EIA)

Customize

Use-case dependent fields: owner/operator, industry, social impacts, timeline, legal and regulatory controls, compliance requirements.

Risk assessment considerations

  • Resource allocation — more risk = more resources. Define scope, identify impacts, determine business value, assign risk level.
  • Risk tolerance — amount of risk the organization is willing and able to endure. Use risk scoring tools to quantify.
  • Risk category — legal, reputational, operational, business, privacy, security. Category may shape response.
  • Impact / risk assessments — identify risks, quantify severity, select controls.

ISO 42005 — AI Impact Assessments

Structured guidance for performing an AIIA.

ISO/IEC 42005:2025 provides structured guidance for performing AI Impact Assessments (AIIA). Identifies, assesses, and documents system impacts.

Purpose

Ensures responsible development and deployment. Identifies risks early in the plan and design stage. Implements controls to mitigate risk. Protects fundamental rights and freedoms. Evaluates personal data use, bias, equitable outcomes. Aligns with organizational standards and legal requirements.

Fair Information Practices (FIPs)

Guidelines for handling data with privacy, security, and fairness.

FIPs are the canonical eight-principle framework for data handling. They predate AI by decades and underpin nearly every modern privacy law.

Disambiguation
FIPs — Fair Information Practices. FIPPs — used to mean both Fair Information Practice Principles and Fair Information Privacy Principles; usage varies by source. FIPS — Federal Information Processing Standards, an unrelated US government standards series. The IAPP material uses FIPs/FIPPs interchangeably.

History

OECD Guidelines (1980) cemented the modern form. Origin: US Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (HEW) Report (1973). The HEW report listed five principles — no secret collection, access and amendment, consent, no secondary use, appropriate safeguards.

Eight common principles

  • Access / Individual participation — right to request access and amend PII; data collected directly from the data subject.
  • Purpose specification — purpose of collection stated.
  • Data minimization / collection limitation — collect only what's necessary; retain only as long as needed.
  • Data quality, relevance — accurate, complete, up-to-date; relevant to specified purpose.
  • Safeguards / security — administrative, technical, physical safeguards.
  • Notice / openness — advance statement; transparency of policies.
  • Accountability — organization owns policies and procedures.
  • Use limitation — used only for specified purpose; no secondary use.
Mnemonic

(Access · Purpose · Data minimization · Data quality · Safeguards · Notice · Accountability · Use limitation)

Applying FIPs to AI

Four AI-specific applications recur in the material:

  • Notice — inform users they're interacting with AI, or that AI is used in processing; disclose whether inputs/outputs are retained for retraining.
  • Choice and consent — voluntary, informed, clearly manifest; plain language; no legalese.
  • Purpose limitation — data used for specified business purpose only.
  • Data minimization — collect only what's necessary; overcollection = liability.

Data controller obligations

Requirements for personal data processors under GDPR.

A data controller — natural or legal person, public authority, or other body — determines the purposes and means of processing personal data. Nine obligation areas follow.

  • PIAs / DPIAs — identify data used to train and operate AI; determine necessity and proportionality; evaluate privacy risks across FIPs, PII usage in (re)training, data sources.
  • Third-party processors — assess risk; ensure compliance; vendor due diligence; contractual agreements covering security and privacy controls; provide notice and obtain consent.
  • Cross-border data transfers — comply with relevant regulations. Under GDPR: Standard Contractual Clauses (SCC) or Binding Corporate Rules (BCR).
  • Data subject rights — restrict processing, not be subject to ADM, data portability, erasure, access and rectification, being informed, objection.
  • Appropriate safeguards — administrative, technical, physical controls; authorized access.
  • Incident management — incident response program; detect, respond, mitigate; investigate and contain.
  • Breach notification — notify affected individuals and authorities; requirements vary by jurisdiction.
  • Record keeping — RoPA, D/PIA, technical documentation, data inventories, maps, data flow diagrams. Supports transparency and accountability principles.

EDPB Opinion 28/2024

When AI models can be considered anonymous; legitimate interest as basis; consequences of unlawful processing.

The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) — collective body of national DPA heads — issued Opinion 28/2024 on data protection aspects of AI models. Three questions, three rulings.

EDPB ensures consistent application of GDPR across the EU/EEA. Originally the WP29; EDPB has a stronger, more independent mandate.

Three rulings

QuestionEDPB position
When can a model be considered anonymous?Models trained on anonymous data are not automatically anonymous. A model is anonymous only if (1) it is impossible to extract original personal data from it, and (2) it does not allow re-identification. If those conditions fail, the model is treated as personal data and is subject to GDPR.
Is legitimate interest a valid basis for developing a model?Yes — but the developer must apply a three-step test: purpose (real, lawful aim), necessity (data processing strictly achieves the aim), and a balancing test (developer's interests must not override fundamental rights and freedoms of data subjects).
If data was processed unlawfully, is the trained model unlawful?Yes, potentially. DPAs may have power to order deletion of the entire model — not just the original training data.

Derivative works

A work based on or derived from one or more existing works. Translations, adaptations, abridgments. AI-generated content built on copyrighted training data has been argued to be derivative.

Fair use

US doctrine permitting limited use of copyrighted material without permission for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, research. Four factors: purpose and character, nature of the work, amount used, effect on market. Fair use is fact-specific; not a defense Claude or any operator should rely on by default.

Model and data licensing

The contractual layer governing model use, training data, and outputs.

Licensing converts IP rights into use rights under defined terms. Licensor grants; licensee receives.

Three license layers in AI

  • Model licenses — terms for using the model itself. Open weights versus closed; commercial use versus research; redistribution rights.
  • Data licenses — terms for training and fine-tuning datasets. Provenance, permissions, restrictions on derivative works.
  • Output licenses — terms for the content the model produces. Who owns outputs? May the licensee resell them?

Contractual controls

Indemnification — one party agrees to compensate the other for specified losses. Common in AI procurement: vendor indemnifies customer against IP infringement claims arising from model outputs. Liability caps, audit rights, kill switches, and AUP enforcement also typical.

Product safety & liability

Existing liability frameworks applied — and stretched — to AI products.

Product safety and liability laws were not developed with AI in mind. AI's autonomy, complexity, and explainability challenges make attributing harm difficult.

Two regimes

  • Product safety/liability laws — hold retailers, distributors, manufacturers responsible for a product's harm.
  • Theories of liability (state-governed) — strict liability (defective product caused harm), negligence (failure to exercise due care), breach of warranty (product promises not met).

Key US statute

Law Consumer Product Safety Act — protects consumers against risk of injury; enables product evaluation; establishes safety standards; promotes research into cause and prevention of injuries and death.

Case studies

  • Case Corelogic — vendor has duty not to sell product that allows user to violate law.
  • Case Meta — software violated the Fair Housing Act.
  • Case Rogers v. Christie (2020) — US District Court (NJ) ruled AI generates information, which does not qualify as a "product" under New Jersey law. Limits scope of strict product liability.

Takeaways

  • Expansion of software vendor liability. Courts may interpret broadly when public policy or civil rights are violated.
  • Ensure documentation supports independent debugging and validation; be cautious about non-compliant configurable options.
  • Document customers' specifications. Include contractual controls, disclaimers, assignment of liability.
  • Vendors may be exempt from liability when the user makes significant modifications or uses the software beyond authorized scope.

Revised Product Liability Directive (PLD)

A 40-year EU framework rewritten for the AI era.

Directive 2024/2853 — the Revised Product Liability Directive — is a major overhaul of 40-year-old laws governing how consumers are compensated for harm. Member states must comply by December 2026.

Purpose

Modernizes the definition of "product." Makes it easier for victims to prove liability and receive compensation when AI causes harm.

Six elements

  • Expanded scope — treats digital products the same as physical. Standalone software, AI-enabled systems, cloud-based AI services, digital platforms. Covers updates, modifications, patches.
  • Burden of proof — "rebuttable presumption of defectiveness." Law automatically assumes a non-compliant product is defective; burden shifts to the manufacturer. Claimants may request technical documentation.
  • Harmonized legislation — consistent rights across the EU. Maintains strict liability regime; victims need not prove negligence.
  • Damage covered — injury, death, property, psychological harm, financial losses from security vulnerability or incorrect AI-driven decisions, data loss or corruption.
  • Liability chain — manufacturers, AI deployers/providers, importers/distributors, third parties.
  • Implications and compliance — robust testing, validation, risk assessment throughout the development life cycle; cybersecurity; documentation and explainability requirements; alignment with EU AI Act.

EU AI Act — objectives, scope, and definitions

What the Act aims for, who it covers, and what it counts as AI.

Objectives are safe, trustworthy, transparent AI that respects fundamental rights. The Act is extraterritorial and applies to providers and users in the EU, providers outside the EU placing products on the EU market, and operators outside the EU producing output used in the EU.

Scope exceptions

Military, national security. Non-professional. Open source (non-high-risk, non-prohibited). Public authorities in third countries, international organizations. R&D (non-commercial, public and private).

Definition of AI system

A machine-based system that operates with autonomy, exhibits post-deployment adaptiveness, infers implicit or explicit objectives from input, generates outputs (predictions, content, recommendations), and influences physical or virtual environments.

Aligns with the OECD definition. Includes deep learning, RLHF, ML, computer vision, NLP, neural networks. Excludes ADM under GDPR with no inferences, and traditional software that doesn't meet the cumulative definition (e.g. expert systems).

AI system ≠ AI model

The model is a component of the system.

GPAI model

General Purpose AI. Also known as foundation model. Trained on "a large amount of data" by self-supervision at scale. Displays significant generality. Performs a range of distinct tasks competently. Integrable into downstream applications. Excludes pre-market AI models used for R&D or prototyping.

EU AI Act — high-risk systems

Most regulation lives here.

High-risk classification depends on specific use. Criteria appear in Article 6(1) and Annex III. A system is high-risk if it meets both 6(1) criteria, or is listed in Annex III.

Article 6(1) criteria

Intended to be used as a safety component of a product — or the system itself is a product covered by "Union harmonisation legislation" listed in Annex I — and required to undergo third-party conformity assessment with a view to placing the system on the market.

EU AI Act — AI literacy (Article 4)

Sufficient AI knowledge across staff, regardless of risk level.

Article 4 requires "a sufficient level of AI literacy of staff and other persons dealing with the operation and use of AI systems on their behalf." Applies to providers and deployers regardless of risk level.

What it is

Skills, knowledge, and understanding that allow providers and deployers to make informed deployment decisions, interpret outputs, and recognize opportunities, risks, and possible harms.

Topics

  • Understanding AI — definitions, ML, deep learning, neural networks; historical development; applications.
  • Technical foundations — algorithms, data, training.
  • Practical skills — using AI for problem-solving and creative tasks; prompt engineering; evaluating outputs.
  • Critical evaluation — presence in different technologies; reliability and biases; risks and benefits.
  • Ethical considerations — bias, fairness, privacy, transparency, explainability, societal impacts.

The EU AI Board supports the European Commission — promotes AI literacy tools and public awareness.

EU AI Act — GPAI models & systemic risk

Chapter V focus: models, not systems.

Chapter V of the EU AI Act focuses on AI models — specifically GPAI models — distinct from the AI systems focus elsewhere.

GPAI with systemic risk

Subset of GPAI. High-impact capabilities determined by technical tools, benchmarks, the Commission, officials, or scientific panel. Definition concerns technical architecture.

Threshold: GPAI is assumed high-impact when training computation exceeds 1025 FLOPs. Article 52(2) creates a rebuttable presumption. At present, systemic risk is determined by computing power, not use case — distinct from the system-level risk approach. Annex XIII adds parameter count, dataset quality and size, input/output modalities, number of users.

Obligations regardless of risk

  • Technical documentation.
  • Transparency information (e.g. model integration).
  • EU copyright law compliance.
  • Summary of training data.
  • Appoint EU representative (for non-EU providers).

Technical documentation

Intended use, AUP, release date, architecture, parameters, modalities, license. Integration information. Training methodologies and techniques. Training, validation, testing data including provenance, curation methodologies, data points, evaluation for biases. Computational resources used for training (FLOPs), total training time. Known or estimated energy consumption.

Exception

Technical documentation and transparency obligations do not apply to providers of open-source GPAI models, as long as the models are adaptable without restriction and details on parameters, weights, architecture, and usage are publicly available. Unless systemic risk applies.

Systemic-risk-specific obligations

  • Perform model evaluation including adversarial testing.
  • Assess and mitigate possible risks at EU level.
  • Track, monitor, report serious incidents without undue delay to AI Office and national authorities. Include corrective measures.
  • Adequate cybersecurity including physical infrastructure protection.

GPAI Code of Practice

Published July 10, 2025. Voluntary tool to help industry comply. Signatories demonstrate compliance by adhering. Three chapters: transparency, copyright, safety and security. Signatories include Amazon, Anthropic, Google, IBM, Microsoft, OpenAI.

Common GPAI obligations across jurisdictions

Documentation. Transparency. Detection tools. Risk management. Human oversight. Incident reporting. Registration and reporting.

Common challenges: data governance; training and fine-tuning; transparency; automated logging and monitoring; third-party tools and integrations.

National and EU-level authorities; tiered penalties.

The Act is enforced at two levels — national (Market Surveillance Authorities and sector-specific authorities) and EU (EDPS and AI Office). This differs from GDPR's single-DPA model.

EU AI Act — supply-chain roles (authorized rep, importer, distributor)

Non-provider operators carry distinct obligations.

Authorized representatives, importers, distributors, and product manufacturers each carry distinct due-diligence, reporting, and cooperation obligations.

Authorized representative — six obligations

  • Mandate — written mandate from provider; provide copy upon request.
  • Due diligence — verify EU declaration of conformity, technical documentation, contact details.
  • Compliance — register high-risk systems in EU database. Must terminate provider mandate if provider isn't fulfilling obligations.
  • Recordkeeping — retain provider's contact details, declaration of conformity, technical documentation, notified body certificate for 10 years.
  • Reporting — when mandate is terminated, notify MSA and notified body with reason.
  • Cooperation — serve as POC for EU national authorities; provide documentation; demonstrate conformity; provide access to logs.

Certificate — issued by a notified body upon successful conformity assessment; evidence of compliance. Notified body — independent organization designated by an EU member state to assess product conformity pre-market.

EU AI Act — deployer obligations + FRIA

Eight deployer obligations, including the Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment.

Deployers carry eight obligation areas plus, for specified systems, a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment.

Eight deployer obligations

  • AI literacy — Article 4 obligations apply.
  • Due diligence — public authorities and EU institutions must verify the AI system is registered in the EU database.
  • Compliance and monitoring — monitor per provider instructions; provide feedback; if use may risk health, safety, or fundamental rights, inform provider/distributor and MSA and suspend use. Carry out DPIA as required by GDPR.
  • Human oversight — assign to a competent natural person; create resources; ensure input data is relevant and representative.
  • Incident reporting — immediately inform provider, importer/distributor, and MSA.
  • Transparency — when deploying in the workplace, employer-deployers must inform affected workers and their representatives. Natural persons must be informed when system use affects fundamental rights.
  • Record-keeping — when processing personal data, retain logs for at least 6 months or as indicated by national law and GDPR.
  • Cooperation with authorities — cooperate with implementation activities; biometric ID system deployers submit annual reports to MSAs and DPAs.

FRIA — Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment

Deployers are responsible, not providers — because deployers determine specific uses. Applies to systems listed in Article 6(2): BC MEEEAL categories, public law entities, private operators of public services (education, healthcare), and private deployers that evaluate creditworthiness or pricing for life and health insurance.

FRIA contents

  • Description of use and intended purpose.
  • Frequency and period of use.
  • Natural persons and groups likely affected.
  • Specific risks and mitigations.
  • Human oversight measures.
  • Governance and compliance mechanisms.

Only required for first use. Must be current. May leverage provider or other existing documentation (e.g. DPIA). Notify MSA of results.

Becoming a provider

Deployers, importers, distributors, or third parties may become providers — subject to Article 16 — when they: put their own name or trademark on a post-market system regardless of contractual arrangements; make substantial modifications; or modify a non-high-risk system that then becomes high-risk.

The new provider replaces the previous provider, but the original provider must cooperate (technical access, compliance, conformity assessment). Original providers can use contractual language prohibiting modification — if changes are still made, the original provider has no obligation to provide technical documentation.

China — regulatory framework

A multi-layered network of laws, regulations, and standards.

OECD AI Classification Framework

Five dimensions for classifying any AI system.

The OECD AI Classification Framework maps any AI deployment along five dimensions to surface policy implications. The OECD is an intergovernmental organization with 38 member countries, founded in 1961, with the objective of stimulating economic progress and world trade.

Five dimensions

  • People and planet — individuals and groups affected (privacy, rights, environment).
  • Economic context — sectors and industries; system scale; business purpose; impact of deployment; AI maturity level.
  • Data and input — training, testing, evaluation data; human expert input for objectives, design, guardrails.
  • AI model — the algorithm and architecture.
  • Tasks and output — what the system actually does and how autonomously.

Why classify?

Allows thinking through policy implications. Promotes common understanding of AI. Informs system inventories. Supports sector-specific frameworks. Supports risk management.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework

Voluntary, rights-preserving, non-sectoral, use-case agnostic.

The NIST AI RMF is the US federal voluntary risk management framework for AI. Required by the National AI Initiative Act (2020). NIST is the National Institute of Standards and Technology — an agency within the Department of Commerce.

Properties

  • Voluntary
  • Rights-preserving
  • Non-sector-specific
  • Use-case agnostic

Related NIST RMFs: NIST SP 800-37, NIST SP 800-53 Revision 5.

Terminology

  • AI actor — organizations and individuals that play an active role in the AI system life cycle.
  • Risk management — coordinated activities to direct and control an organization with regards to risk.
  • Risk tolerance — readiness to bear risk to achieve objectives. Highly contextual; influenced by legal and regulatory requirements.
  • Use-case profile — implementation of the AI RMF for a specific setting or application.
  • TEVV — Testing, Evaluation, Verification, Validation. Verification: meets internal requirement. Validation: meets external customer/stakeholder needs.

Two parts

Part 1 — foundational information: characteristics of trustworthy AI; how organizations can frame AI-related risks.

Part 2 — "core" of the framework: Govern, Map, Measure, Manage.

Seven characteristics of trustworthy AI

  • Valid and reliable
  • Safe
  • Secure and resilient
  • Accountable and transparent
  • Explainable and interpretable
  • Privacy-enhanced
  • Fair, with harmful bias mitigated

Risk management challenges

Third-party software, hardware, data. Tracking emergent risks. Reliable metrics availability. Real-world setting. Inscrutability (black box). Human baseline (difficult to compare human performance to AI). Risk tolerance and prioritization. Organizational integration and management of risk.

The Core — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage

  • Govern — culture of risk management; cross-cutting; accountability structures; workforce diversity; policies for third parties and supply chain.
  • Map — context recognized; categorization performed; capabilities, usage, goals, benefits, costs compared with benchmarks; impacts characterized.
  • Measure — methods and metrics identified and applied; evaluated for trustworthy characteristics; tracking mechanisms; feedback gathered.
  • Manage — respond to risks; maximize benefits; manage third-party risks; document response, recovery, communication.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — AI Management System

The certifiable standard for AI governance.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 establishes an AI Management System (AIMS) — a "Type A" standard certifiable by a third party. Industry and jurisdiction agnostic. Harmonized structure aligns terminology with other ISO/IEC standards.

ISO is the International Organization for Standardization — independent, non-governmental, with representatives from national standards organizations. Founded 1947. Over 25,000 standards published; over 800 technical committees. The IEC is the International Electrotechnical Commission — international standards for electrical and electronic technologies; headquartered in Geneva.

Seven requirements — Part 1

  1. Context — determine organization's role (provider, deployer, importer, distributor); determine stakeholders and their needs.
  2. Leadership — establish AI policies; define roles, responsibilities, authorities.
  3. Planning — processes to identify, assess, mitigate risk; conduct AI impact assessments; assess downstream effects; identify foreseeable misuse.
  4. Support — appropriately resource AI; competency requirements (qualifications, training); training and awareness; internal and external communications.
  5. Operations — identify, assess, mitigate risk; document the risk management process.
  6. Evaluation — internal audit program; monitor and measure AIMS performance; periodic management review. Flexible — organizations determine metrics.
  7. Improvement — continuous monitoring and improvement.

Nine controls — Part 2

  • AI impact assessments — establish process; document.
  • AI development life cycle — responsible planning, design, development, deployment; verification mechanisms; deployment plan; continuous monitoring; technical documentation.
  • Data — governance processes covering acquisition, quality, provenance, preparation.
  • Documentation — keep records to update stakeholders.
  • Policies — for development and use.
  • Roles, responsibilities, and reporting — define; implement incident-reporting process.
  • Resources — determine across the entire life cycle (data, tools, hardware, talent).
  • Responsible use — objectives and process defined.
  • Third-parties and users — assign responsibilities to AI operators throughout the supply chain.

ISO 31000:2018 — Risk Management Guidelines

Sector-agnostic risk management.

ISO 31000:2018 is the family of risk management standards. Objective: consistent vocabulary and methodology for assessing and managing risk.

Scope

Applicable and adaptable to any public, private, or community enterprise. Not developed for any particular sector. Best-practice structure and guidance for all risk management operations.

Eight principles

  • Continuous improvement
  • Customized
  • Inclusive
  • Integrated
  • Dynamic
  • Best available information
  • Human and cultural factors
  • Structured and comprehensive

Six leadership principles

  • Leadership · Integration · Design · Implementation · Evaluation · Improvement

Risk management process

Identify risks. Evaluate risk probability. Determine risk severity. Implement mitigations.

IEEE 7000-21

A model process for addressing ethical concerns during system design.

IEEE 7000-21 is the Standard Model Process for Addressing Ethical Concerns during System Design. The IEEE is the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers — the world's largest technical organization, over 400,000 members in 160 countries.

Scope

All organizations. "Ethical": supporting the realization of positive values or reduction of negative values.

Objectives

  • Design systems while considering individual and societal ethical values.
  • Balance management commitments (time, budget) with social responsiveness and accountability.
  • Ensure management and engineering communication with stakeholders.
  • Integrate traceability of ethical values in operations.

Human values

  • Transparency · Sustainability · Privacy · Fairness and accountability

System values

  • Efficiency · Effectiveness

HUDERAF

Human Rights, Democracy, and the Rule of Law Assurance Framework for AI Systems.

HUDERAF is the Council of Europe's framework for assessing AI impact on human rights, democracy, and the rule of law.

Disambiguation
Council of Europe — international organization upholding human rights, democracy, and the rule of law in Europe. 46 member states. Cannot legislate; can advocate for enforcement of international agreements.
European Commission — part of the EU executive. Operates as cabinet government. 27 members headed by a President. Administrative body of ~32,000 civil servants.
HUDERAF was created by the Council of Europe — not the European Commission.

What it is

Created by the Council of Europe's Ad hoc Committee on Artificial Intelligence. Focus on human rights and laws. Basis for legal framework. Created the HUDERIA — Human Rights, Democracy, and the Rule of Law Impact Assessment.

Objective

Define and develop impact assessments that follow the proportionality principle — risk management and stakeholder involvement proportionate to the likelihood and extent of risks. Incorporate human rights with AI-centered approaches. Risk-based. Develop methods to assess and grade likelihood of risks.

Eight principles

  • Human dignity
  • Human freedom and autonomy
  • Prevention of harm
  • Non-discrimination
  • Transparency and explainability
  • Data protection and right to privacy
  • Democracy
  • Rule of law

Risk management process

  • Identify human rights likely impacted.
  • Assess impact on rights.
  • Assess governance mechanisms in place to mitigate risks, get stakeholder involvement, provide remedy, ensure accountability.
  • Monitor and evaluate.

Colophon & ambiguity notes

Source treatment and two flagged ambiguities.

This study script consolidates Domain 1 and Domain 2 lecture material into a single reference. Two ambiguities in the source were preserved and flagged rather than silently resolved.

Ambiguity 1 — FIPs / FIPPs / FIPS

The source decks use FIPs and FIPPs interchangeably to refer to Fair Information Practices / Fair Information Practice Principles. FIPS is a different acronym (Federal Information Processing Standards) and should not be conflated. The glossary uses FIPs as the primary term; FIPPs is treated as a synonym.

Ambiguity 2 — BC MEEEAL mnemonic

The high-risk mnemonic lists nine domains (biometrics; critical infrastructure; migration; education; employment; essential services; administration of justice; law enforcement) using eight letters. Treated as a coverage mnemonic rather than a strict letter map. Memorize the full list; the letters are for recall scaffolding only.

  • For mapped concepts (risk pyramid, 6(3) exceptions, FRIA contents), draw the structure on paper.
